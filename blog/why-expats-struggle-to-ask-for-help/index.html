Why Expats Struggle to Ask for Help

By Danielle Leite · 25 May 2026 · 5 min read

You moved abroad for adventure, opportunity, or love. So why does it feel so hard to admit you're struggling? If this sounds familiar, you're not alone. It's one of the most common things I hear from expats in my counselling practice.

For many people living abroad, asking for help feels like admitting defeat. You chose this life, after all. You're supposed to be grateful, adventurous, brave. And most of the time, you are. But that doesn't mean it's easy.

The independence trap

The very qualities that got you abroad — independence, resilience, adaptability — can also make it harder to recognise when you need support. You've built a life from scratch in another country. You've navigated bureaucracy in a foreign language, found a home, made friends, figured out healthcare systems that make no sense. You're resourceful. You get things done.

So when the loneliness creeps in, or the anxiety builds, or your relationship starts to crack under the weight of all the change, it can feel like a personal failure. You think: I should be able to handle this. Other people seem fine. What's wrong with me?

Nothing is wrong with you. Living abroad is one of the most psychologically complex experiences a person can have, and struggling with it doesn't mean you're weak. It means you're human.

The "lucky" narrative

There's a story that follows expats everywhere: you're so lucky. And in many ways, you are. But that narrative creates a kind of emotional cage. If everyone keeps telling you how wonderful your life is, how do you say, actually, I'm not okay?

Many expats carry a quiet guilt about their struggles. They compare themselves to people who didn't have the opportunity to live abroad, or to refugees and migrants who had no choice. They minimise their own pain because they feel they don't have the right to it.

But pain isn't a competition. Your experience is valid, and your struggles deserve attention regardless of how they compare to someone else's.

The support gap

When you live in your home country, you usually have a network: family nearby, friends you've known for years, a GP who knows your history, maybe even a therapist you've seen before. When you move abroad, most or all of that disappears overnight.

Building a new support network takes time — often years. And in the meantime, many expats cope by keeping busy, staying positive, and pushing through. It works, until it doesn't.

When to reach out

If any of these feel familiar, it might be time to talk to someone:

  • You feel lonely even when you're surrounded by people
  • You're irritable, anxious, or tearful more often than usual
  • You've lost interest in things that used to bring you joy
  • You feel disconnected from your partner, your friends, or yourself
  • You keep thinking about going "home" but you're not sure where that is anymore
  • You're going through a major life change — a divorce, a job loss, a move — and you feel overwhelmed

These aren't signs of weakness. They're signs that you're carrying more than you should have to carry alone.

You don't have to do this by yourself

Asking for help isn't giving up. It's one of the bravest things you can do. And working with a counsellor who understands the expat experience — someone who's lived it — can make all the difference.

I've been an expat for over 30 years. I know what it's like to feel lost in a country that's supposed to be home. And I know how much it helps to have someone in your corner who truly gets it.
